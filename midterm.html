David Wojnarowicz

David Wojnarowicz (VOY-nə-ROH-vitch; September 14, 1954 - July, 1992) was an American painter, photographer, writer, filmmaker, performance artist and AIDS activist notable in the New York City art world. Born in Red Bank, New Jersey, he lived most of his life in New York City, where he attended the High School of Performing Arts. His parents divorced when he was 2, leaving him to a life filled with temporary homes and abusive relationships. During his teenage years he was a street hustler. He graduated from the High School of Music and Art in Manhattan.


In the 1990s, Wojnarowicz sued and issued an injunction against Donald Wildmon and the American Family Association on the grounds that Wojnarowicz's work had been copied in violation of the New York Artists' Authorship Rights Act.

Biography

He returned to New York City in the late 1970s and quickly became a prolific member of an avant-garde movement, which used mixed media as well as used graffiti and street art.


He made super-8 films, such as Heroin and Beautiful People. He also began a photographic series of Arthur Rimbaud and played in a band know as 3 Teens Kill 4 who released their independent EP No Motive in 1982. He exhibited his work in East Village galleries such as Civilian Warfare, Ground Zero Gallery NY, Public Illumination Picture Gallery, Gracie Mansion and Hal Bromm.


David Wojnarowicz also collaborated on works with artists such as:

  • Nan Goldin
  • Peter Hujar
  • Luis Frangella
  • Karen Finley
  • Marguerite Van Cook
  • Ben Neill
In 1987 his mentor and lover, Peter Hujar, died of AIDS. Later, David learned that he was HIV-positive. After Peter Hujar's death, Wojnarowicz's work moved towards more explicit political content and activist themes, usually centering around social and legal injustice apparent during the AIDS epidemic.


Wojnarowicz died in Manhattan home on July 22, 1992 from what was later confirmed as AIDS.

In Spring 2011, the P.P.O.W. gallery showed Spirituality, which was an exhibition of Wojnarowicz's drawings, photographs, videos, and personal notebooks. A retrospective, David Wojnarowicz: History Keeps Me Awake at Night, was announced for exhibition at the Whitney Museum of American Art in spring 2018. It was co-curated by David Kiehl and David Breslin and received international acclaim.

He was also the author of several book. Each often centered around the political and social issues of the 1980s specifically about the AIDS epidemic. One of his books,Close to the Knives: A Memoir of Disintegration, is an autobiography discussing topics such as his tumultuous childhood, his art career in New York City, and being diagnosed with AIDS. Though his artwork was main way he expressed himself, he used books and other media as a way to speak about the controversies during that time period.
